Beautiful Errors

Exhibition curated by Ethan Lee

Curatorial Statement

Beautiful Errors explores what happens when technology fails beautifully. I’ve always been drawn to the moments when screens freeze, files corrupt, and code collapses—the small glitches that remind us there’s a human hand behind the machine. The artists in this show embrace those digital flaws as creative tools. JODI uses code itself as visual language, Mark Napier’s Shredder deconstructs the web’s clean surfaces, and Nathalie Lawhead’s Tetrageddon Games invites players into a joyful kind of computer chaos. Together, these works reveal the emotional side of glitch: how systems can misbehave, and in doing so, feel strangely alive. I’m also interested in how AI now tries to perfect what glitch artists deliberately break—this site plays with that tension through flicker, motion, and layout “errors” that make the exhibition itself part of the artwork.

Works in the Show

jodi.org — 1995–present

JODI (Joan Heemskerk & Dirk Paesmans)

A foundational work of net art that uses HTML and browser code as visual material. The “broken” look is intentional, turning the act of browsing into an unpredictable performance.

Visit jodi.org

Tetrageddon Games — 2012–ongoing

Nathalie Lawhead (alienmelon)

An interactive net-art universe filled with pop-ups, windows, and playful chaos. Lawhead turns computer overload into humor and emotion, celebrating the messiness of digital life.

Explore Tetrageddon

The Shredder — 1998

Mark Napier

An early interactive net artwork that “shreds” any website entered by the user, turning HTML into abstract color and form—revealing the web’s hidden structure as art.

Use The Shredder

Design intentionally includes motion and flicker to echo glitch aesthetics.
